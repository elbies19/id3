Useful Tools for Your Teaching
The internet has countless tools available for students and teachers alike — here are some curated favorites for you (and your students) to try.
Writing & Editing

Free and low-cost tools that can be integrated into composition instruction or shared directly with students:

  • Ellipsus

    A collaborative writing platform built specifically for creative and long-form writing, with connected drafts, real-time collaboration, and feedback tools in one place. Notable for its explicit no-generative-AI stance, which makes it a useful recommendation for students who want a distraction-free writing environment without AI suggestions baked in.

  • Grammarly

    A writing assistant that checks grammar, clarity, tone, and style in real time. The free tier is fine for most student use cases.

    NOTE: Worth recommending with a caveat in your syllabus about over-reliance — it's most useful as a proofreading pass, not a substitute for revision.

  • LibreOffice

    A free, fully-featured alternative to Microsoft Office that students can install on any device. Useful to know about for students who don't have Office access at home.

  • GPTZero Origin

    A Chrome extension from GPTZero that creates a real-time time-lapsed video of a document's creation while flagging suspected AI use. Useful for building AI awareness habits rather than enforcement.

Research

Free and low-cost tools for supporting student research, source evaluation, and citation management:

  • Checkology by the News Literacy Project

    A free virtual classroom platform focused on media literacy and source evaluation. Particularly useful for research units — the modules on identifying misinformation and evaluating sources translate directly to composition skills.

  • EBSCOhost at SCC

    SCC's library database, available to all enrolled students. Contact the library for access instructions and subject-specific guides.

  • Zotero

    Free citation management tool that integrates with most browsers and word processors. Genuinely useful for research-heavy assignments and worth teaching as a transferable skill.

Technology

Tools for accessibility, multimedia, and course infrastructure:

  • ASU Online Image Accessibility Creator

    A free AI-powered tool from Arizona State University that generates alt text for images from a simple file upload. Useful for accessibility remediation — if you're adding images to your Canvas course and need a quick starting point for alt text, this gets you most of the way there with minimal effort.

  • Canvas Studio

    SCC's built-in video tool inside Canvas, available to all instructors and students. Useful for video assignment submissions, instructor feedback via timestamped comments, and asynchronous discussion alternatives.

Teaching

Resources for instructional design, active learning, and assessment:

  • eLearningDesign

    A collection of free interactive activity generators — timelines, sorting activities, matching exercises, and more — that produce embeddable HTML with no account required. Low lift for adding interactivity to a Canvas page.

  • McGraw Center for Teaching & Learning (Princeton) Digital Toolkit

    A curated collection of teaching resources from Princeton's faculty development center, covering active learning, discussion facilitation, assessment design, and more. Institutional context aside, the resources are broadly applicable and freely available.

  • UPenn CETLI Online Assessment Tools

    A resource library from Penn's teaching center focused specifically on online and hybrid assessment strategies. Worth bookmarking for anyone rethinking how they assess writing in a digital environment.

Student Engagement

Tools for collaborative learning, annotation, and peer interaction:

  • Padlet

    A flexible digital bulletin board tool for collaborative brainstorming, discussion, and sharing. Free tier has some limitations but is workable for most classroom uses. Good for low-stakes warm-ups, peer sharing, and visual organization activities.

  • Perusall

    A social annotation platform that lets students read and annotate texts collaboratively, with instructor visibility into engagement. Integrates with Canvas. Particularly well-suited for close reading assignments and building discussion around shared texts before class.
